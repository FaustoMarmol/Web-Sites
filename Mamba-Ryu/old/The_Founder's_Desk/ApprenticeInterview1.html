Apprentice Interview I
Insights Into The Fire And Wind Spheres

Apprentice:
Five Spheres encompass the art of Mamba-Ryu.  The Earth Sphere (Black Mamba) fashions the body into a versatile and deadly weapon.  The Water Sphere (Green Mamba) makes the student into a Master of survival, an autonomous unit able to independently thrive in any of the world’s habitats, including and perhaps most importantly the ‘urban jungle.’  This interview pertains to two of the lesser known Spheres of Mamba, the realm of the more advanced practitioner:  the Fire Sphere pertains to the training of the conscious mind and is learned by studying the discipline of Mamba Mindfulness; the Wind Sphere trains the nonconscious mind and is learned by studying Hypno-Shamanism.  Only after achieving mastery of these Spheres can a student hope to attain the Void Sphere, which represents the fusion of the other four into a comprehensive understanding, a state that like Enlightenment cannot be trained, only attained.

Founder:
My first comment would be that there isn’t exactly a relationship of equivalency between the Wind Sphere and Hypno-Shamanism.  It is the case that Imaginoceptive Meta-Programming is something that falls under the general area of the Wind Sphere, however you have to keep in mind that every Sphere contains elements of each and every other Sphere.  So in a sense the five Spheres of Mamba-Ryu have what is referred to as a ‘fractal design,’ where in a fractal a component contains all aspects of every other component, all the way down.  So Mamba Mindfulness, for example, which you are correct in stating is the primary domain/area of study within the Fire Sphere, is something that once it’s ingrained also applies to the Wind Sphere, because how to think mindfully or how to control the conscious mind then becomes a non-conscious process, and that is the mastery a student hopes to achieve.

So regarding the division in Spheres, think of it more as a guideline to help people grasp the somewhat elusive paradigm that Mamba-Ryu presents for those who are accustomed to categories being well-defined.  But really the Spheres go beyond categories – you can think of them more in terms of ‘tendencies.’  The Earth Sphere deals primarily with physical combat, with developing the body into a weapon – but it is not a mind-less weapon, it is a mind-full weapon; it is not a weapon without intent, or a weapon without an awareness of how and when it should be applied.

Apprentice:
How will one who has attained mastery of the Fire Sphere exhibit it as he trains the Earth Sphere?  How will he exhibit it as he goes about his daily affairs?

Founder:
The Fire Sphere focuses primarily on strategy, strategic thinking – how to think about thinking.  Mindfulness is an important aspect of it, but in general it addresses how to think strategically, how to solve problems, how to break problems down.  An individual who has attained a degree of adeptness, a certain proficiency in the Fire Sphere is going to be more strategic, is going to be more tactical in their approach; they’re going to be more mindful of what they should be mindful of – aspects of the opponent, aspects of themselves.  They’ll have a higher degree of analysis and a more proficient control over the effort expended – higher efficiency and higher efficacy.  Having said that, what one must realize is that whereas the direct application that one would assume of the Earth Sphere is hand-to-hand combat, for the most part in one’s life there is a very, very small aspect that could threaten your existence.  The Fire Sphere is much more focused on how to deal strategically with adversity.  So really you have to invert the order of those things and think that ‘alright, the Earth Sphere isn’t just for making the body into a weapon, but it’s more of a metaphor for the rest of your existence that doesn’t involve necessarily hand-to-hand combat but does involve adversity in general.’  And adversity can of course encompass situations with ‘adversaries,’ but adversity does not require a physical or a tangible opponent – it can consist of attitudes in ourselves, behaviors that are unwanted, belief systems, schemas; the Fire Sphere applies to anything that requires the overcoming of adversity.  When you are training the higher level of the Earth Sphere, i.e. the kind of level that someone who is proficient in the Fire Sphere would bring to it, you would be looking at things not so concretely in terms of a hand-to-hand combat combative situation, but would instead relate it to the much broader and (as far as Mamba-Ryu is concerned) much more important aspect of life in general – dealing with adversity.

How would he exhibit it going about his daily affairs?  Well, one would pause at the fundamental axiom of Mamba-Ryu, and that is – life contains adversity.  Adversity is simply inherent to existence, to being a living being; it is not because we’ve committed some kind of sin or ‘fallen out of grace’ with the gods or the universe or inherited sins of our forefathers or reincarnated from another existence with some kind of state of detriment for which we are accountable – it is simply a fact of being alive.  You will encounter adversity.  So first of all, one who is adept at the Fire Sphere will have the attitude that adversity is something to be expected, something to be dealt with in a methodical way, as opposed to something that you think you’re being punished for or that you hope to avoid in life – it’s just something that you’re going to encounter, so you seek the rules and principles that apply to overcome the adversity.  You’re going to examine your resources and how best to deploy those resources for a resolution to the situation that is most favorable to you. 

Apprentice:
How will one who has attained mastery of the Wind Sphere exhibit it as he trains the Earth Sphere?  How will he exhibit it as he goes about his daily affairs?

Founder:
The domain of the Wind Sphere is the non-conscious mind – that aspect of ourselves which is the reservoir of our habits, of our emotions, of the reasons why we do the things we do when there are no reasons; it is the repository of the irrational which is what defines human beings and therefore human existence.  So when someone who is an adept of the Wind Sphere is practicing the Earth Sphere, they’re going to be focusing on the non-conscious of both themselves and their opponents: ‘what does your opponent do that they’re not aware of the fact that they’re doing?  Do they have habits?  Do they have patterns?  Do you have habits and patterns that you’re not aware of?’  And you’re going to focus on ‘well, how do I identify those habits and those patterns that are presenting themselves in my behavior and that are detrimental to me – how do I identify them and how do I go about modifying/correcting them in some way?’  And as far as your opponent – ‘how can I identify them and exploit them to my advantage?’  How do you identify the patterns that your opponent isn’t aware of and how do you exploit them? 

This breakdown, ‘conscious/non-conscious mind,’ ‘Fire Sphere/Wind Sphere,’ gives a sense of a model of the mind in operation.  The model of the mind is essential to understanding human existence, or how humans exist in this world – we do not exist outside of our minds, outside of our thoughts, of our behaviors, of how we think and how we imagine and how we feel.  So the same kind of approach that the adept took to combat training in which he or she tries to identify which patterns are not working, modify them, improve on them and create new patterns, he or she would also look at their life in general – ‘am I where I want to be?  Should I want to be where I am?’ – and realize that most of our behavior is non-conscious in nature.  If the answer is ‘no,’ they’re going to want to identify ‘well, what are those patterns?  What are those habits?  What are those inclinations?  What are those emotions that rule me, that impulse me, that to a great degree have programmed me, that have led me to the position that I am in?’  Taking stock of those issues may require them to hire a sounding board (which is largely what a therapist does for example or what an Existential Strategist does) to sort of help work these things out, to help identify these patterns; and after having identified them ‘well, how do I modify them?  How do I modify them in order to facilitate my transition from where I am to where I want to be?’  Also in the process of analyzing ‘where am I?’ is: ‘what’s taken me to this point?  What are these non-conscious habits, patterns, decisions, emotions and all these things that have directed my behavior?  What are they?  How do I get in touch with them, how do I identify them, how do I modify them?’

Apprentice:
What Fire Sphere and Wind Sphere skills will one indirectly develop by training the Earth Sphere?  Give an example or two please.

Founder:
A more appropriate question would be: ‘how do the skills, techniques and performance objectives of the Fire Sphere and Wind Sphere translate into physical combative applications?’ because essentially that is what we are looking at.  Now, inasmuch as the Wind Sphere has to do with training the nonconscious mind, when you are in a combative situation the ideal is to train a series of responses to the point at which they are no longer conscious.  In other words, to the point at which they are simply intuitive, reflexive – conscious thoughts do not need to mediate your responses to a given situation.  You respond without thought.  So they are in a sense ‘responses without a responder’ because the responding is not mediated by any kind of intentionality, ‘conscious’ intentionality, even though the action itself will be attributed to the individual who is objectively seen as carrying it out.  But in essence, you just do things without even being aware of the fact that you’re doing them.  That is the ideal.  And inasmuch as that transfers to physical combat that is what you’re looking for.  You’re looking to be so trained that for any given situation you respond without having to think. 

As far as ‘what aspects of the Fire Sphere will be developed in the Earth Sphere?’ we’re looking at essentially a representation of the same points.  What is the Fire Sphere all about?  It’s about strategic thinking, tactical thinking.  It’s about training the conscious mind, in a sense, to think and to strategize, to plan, to be tactical, automatically.  From the standpoint of the Earth Sphere, which is again combative, you’re trying to elevate your application of techniques beyond simple kick and punch, parry, bob and weave, into a level in which you are implementing some kind of strategy, some kind of tactical representation.  You’re not just being reactive in an immediate sense to the smaller, lower level aspects of the situation, but you’re taking a broader perspective in mind.  Let’s say, for example, an encounter is beginning to present itself – it looks like it’s developing; if you’re thinking strategically you would be thinking about positioning.  Where is the best position for you to be in to respond to the event?  If it’s at night and there is a source of light, well you know that you’d want to be with your back to the source of light so that you’d benefit visually from the light shining on your opponent and yet your opponent would be hampered by the light shining in their faces, reducing their visibility.  Positioning.  If the encounter is taking place in a bar you might want to subtly position yourself where you have access to improvised weapons: a chair, a bottle, a pool stick, a pool ball.  If it’s a restaurant: well, a fork, a knife.  If it’s a hallway in a hotel then maybe you’d want to be near the fire alarm, or better yet the fire alarm with the axe to break the glass.  So you could be thinking in terms of positioning.  You would want to automatically bring all those aspects of tactical and strategic thinking to the combative situation at hand.  Basically, real-life hand-to-hand combat differs significantly from a ring.  A ring is a ring is a ring; it has a limited number of areas – you’re in the middle of the ring, you’re on the ropes, you’re in the corner.  Versus real life – there are varying configurations of three-dimensional space, so if you’re thinking strategically you would think ‘how can I position myself to take advantage of those configurations?’


Apprentice:
How does the discipline of Existential Strategy fit into the Fire and Wind Spheres?  Does it simply utilize a more mundane subject matter to both harbor peace within the conscious mind (Fire Sphere) and the removal of anxiety from the nonconscious mind (Wind Sphere), thereby facilitating mastery of Mamba Mindfulness and Hypno-Shamanism?  Also, how does it stand alone as a discipline?

Founder:
Well let me answer the last part first because it reflects upon the rest of the question.  Essentially what you have with Existential Strategy is ‘how to live.’  How to live your life; how to be; how to have an existence.  What are the strategies?  Inasmuch as ‘strategy’ as a term comes from the Greek strategos, strategoi, meaning General and Generalship, how do you become the General of your existence?  What is the art of being the General of your existence?  With that in mind – ‘what is the strategy?’  Well, it’s a plan – a methodology – to obtain an objective with limited available resources.  Existence has many different facets and many different planes of which physical combat tends to factor minimally.  That is, unless you live a sociopathic existence as a gang member or a Mafioso, or you’re a soldier (and even then soldiers for the most part spend the majority of their time training rather than actually fighting) – for most people in the civilized world physical combat is a limited aspect of their lives.  So, Existential Strategy addresses what else goes on in someone’s life: there are relationships, there are adversities having to do with one’s job, having to do with practically anything.  It is the art of being and the art of applying strategic principles and a strategic mindset to being and to existence.  Central to that, first and foremost, are issues of identity – who are you?  And that is a huge topic in and of its own. 

Now, how does Existential Strategy stand apart from Mamba-Ryu per se?  Well, it does and it does not.  It does not in that Mamba-Ryu is all about Existential Strategy.  It’s about ‘who are you?’  ‘Where are you?’  ‘Who do you want to be’ and ‘how do you get to where you want to be?’  Now, when you operate as an Existential Strategist you are trying to help other people answer those questions and reach those objectives, but within a different framework.  So, beginning with the notion of identity, the fundamental framework of identity of a Mamba is different or could be different from that of a Hindu or that of a Muslim or that of a Christian or any number of people.  However, the questions, the issues, the problems that they face within their given framework are very similar.  So they would benefit from applying strategy.  They would benefit from wrestling/struggling with the question of identity:  ‘who am I as a Muslim?’  ‘Who am I as a Christian?’  ‘Who am I as a Mamba?’  The Existential Strategist therefore can assist other people with the questions, keeping in mind their fundamental frameworks and allowing them also an opportunity to question them.  Because if one did not question those frameworks then what would be the point of faith?  Faith without questioning is really stupidity – it’s just ignorance; unless you have an option then what is the value of your selection?  Nothing.  “You can have any color car you want to as long as it’s black.”  Well, the whole issue of the opportunity to select is central to faith.  If you believe in black cars then that’s only meaningful if there are white cars and red cars and blue cars and green cars and silver cars to choose from.  So, Existential Strategy therefore can have two aspects: you can work with people (as I do) and help them answer the six fundamental existential questions: ‘who am I?’  ‘Who do I want to be?’ ‘How do I become that?’  ‘Where am I?’  ‘Where do I want to be?’  ‘How do I get there?’ within their own given framework, or you can address these questions within the Mamba-Ryu paradigm itself.  Then all that remains is to operate within that framework.


Apprentice:
The Fire Sphere and Wind Sphere lead the student along a path to first understanding his mind, learning to control his mind, and finally to mastering it.  Is understanding our own mind essential to understanding the enemy’s?  In what way does it facilitate the process?

Founder:
Well the most important thing to keep in mind (no pun intended) is that the principal enemy is oneself.  The enemy lies within.  So inasmuch as that is the true enemy, the answer would be: well yeah, you have to understand your own mind in order to understand your enemy’s mind because the real enemy lies within.  Understanding the nature of the mind is the first challenge, the great step in the right direction.  And it is one of the more controversial aspects of human learning because it is intimately related to the issue of identity – ‘who am I? what am I?’ etc.  The nature of the mind is enshrouded in the nature of other (associated) issues such as the soul.  Even in psychology the term suka (psyche), which is the mind, is also the soul in Greek; for them the mind was the soul.  Now, it becomes controversial for people who believe that the soul is immortal, that the soul is something separate from aspects of the material body; the model that they’re going to have of the mind is radically different.  In order to understand something you have to begin by asking yourself, ‘well, what is its essence?  What does it consist of?’  Now, if you’re coming from for example a more neurocognitive perspective in which mind is essentially a manifestation of what the brain does, even though we don’t understand fully what the brain does, then mind becomes something akin to the gravitational field associated with matter or the electromagnetic field associated with an alternating current flowing through an electrical circuit; mind would be another such elemental field or elemental ‘something’ that is not really a substance but is just something unique unto itself.  When you have a complex central nervous system (such as human), you have a mind.  So, parting from that, you could then start building an understanding of what mind is.  However, if you believe that the mind is synonymous with the soul which is independent of the body, then you’re going to have a very, very different understanding of what is mind and therefore how to go about disciplining it. 


Apprentice:
Give a rough, general curriculum for the Wind Sphere.

Founder:
If we’re following the general methodology of Imaginoceptive Meta-Programming, also known as Hypnotic Shamanism, then the first thing is being able to relax one’s body and relax one’s conscious mind sufficiently to enable the nonconscious mind to manifest itself – what we would commonly call a ‘trance state.’  Although this kind of communication or manifestation of the nonconscious mind does not have to be in a trance state, it is facilitated by the nature of the trance state.  In any case, the program would first entail allowing oneself to enter into this state.  One would develop the capacity to enter into it readily, fully, to begin to experience what are so-called imaginals, which are just a manifestation of the dreamscape – entities that are apparently autonomous, that have their own volition.  For example, it’s akin to when you are asleep and dreaming – the cast of characters that appear in the dreamscape are not something that you’re consciously controlling, consciously moving; on the contrary they are independent of your ‘self,’ of your ‘will,’ of your ‘conscious will.’  But they are of course manifestations of your nonconscious mind.  Keep in mind that in a Shamanic culture the beliefs could be such that one is actually in a different dimension of reality and that these are actually autonomous entities.  In short, in the trance state you would want to develop to the point where you have imaginals, you have a dreamscape, and you have interaction with those imaginals.  The imaginals are representations/manifestations of your nonconscious mind, and you soon learn to establish a free and open communication with the nonconscious mind by way of using these imaginals. 

The next level could be (for example) the capacity to experience shape-shifting along with a familiar – your power animal.  You would identify your power animal, which may be a tiger, a wolf, a horse, etc., and then shape-shift in the trance state into that power animal, thus adopting the positive qualities that you attribute to that animal, and finally carrying on those qualities into the rest of your existence.  It’s a very powerful shamanistic technique.  And we see this technique expressed for example in kung fu in the different animal forms, in which one not only executes techniques and tactics attributed to these animals but also psychological characteristics of the animals themselves.  So that would be sort of the outline for a basic, fundamental and essential development of the Wind Sphere using the technique that we refer to as Imaginoceptive Meta-Programming, also known as Hypnotic Shamanism.


Apprentice:
The conscious mind can be used to effect change and ‘reprogram’ the nonconscious mind, and vice versa.  So it seems that there would be considerable overlap in the scope (and therefore the training) of the Fire and Wind Spheres.  If a student studied both Spheres, what understanding could he expect to achieve at each ‘stage’ as he slowly progressed to become a Master?

Founder:
First of all we must come to terms with the fact that the mere words that we use are representations, and not very good ones at that.  We really lack the proper terminology to understand the mind.  So when we speak of ‘conscious mind’ and ‘nonconscious mind’ the impression that it gives is that they are two separate and distinct entities, two separate and distinct faculties.  But really they are not.  They are simply two different phases or expressions of something that the brain does that we call ‘mind.’  Having said that, of course there’s going to be considerable overlap between the two. 

Let me give you an example of what conscious/nonconscious processes are.  A conscious process would be learning to drive a standard, a stick shift.  Once you get down the movements, the synchronization and the timing of the clutch and the gears and the accelerator and so on, and you no longer need to think about it, then you’re able to carry out other functions and think about other things while a different aspect of the nonconscious mind (as we refer to it) can carry out the once-trying task of shifting gears and driving the vehicle.  Now, experimentally there was a researcher (from the University of Oregon, I believe) who used a functional Magnetic Resonant Imaging paradigm to show how initially when we learn a task areas of the frontal lobes (the areas basically behind your forehead) are engaged, they are heavily activated as one carries out the task – because you need to think about it.  However, once the task becomes more automatic, once the task becomes something that you can just do, and do while your attention is diverted somewhere else, then the activation moved away from the frontal areas towards so-called parietal areas (which is kind of towards the rear of your skull).  So, different parts of the brain are now engaged in performing the task; what your brain is trying to do, essentially, is designate tasks that you don’t want to have to think about (that you want to be automatic) to the nonconscious mind.  However, you want to have the power of override because sometimes there are habits that you want to change – behaviors that are automatic that you want to be aware of, examine and reprogram.  In effect, you want to consciously be aware of those tasks so you can examine them – you want to take them out of their state of automatic processing, for lack of a better term, to monitor them, scrutinize them, make the necessary adjustments, cut out the bad habits, become more effective, more efficient in our movements, in our patterns of conduct of behavior, and then reintegrate them into our wide variety of nonconscious tasks that we carry out daily.  Most people can chew gum and walk at the same time without tripping or choking on their gum. 

One who has developed a certain amount of expertise in managing the conscious and nonconscious processes would, for one thing, monitor their mind; they develop a mindfulness, a meta-consciousness, a meta-awareness of what they are doing in certain circumstances.  And they would be examining not just their conscious processes but their nonconscious ones – those which are automatic – and try to become aware of them to ask themselves ‘is this appropriate?  Is this what I should be doing?’  And if not, they make a conscious change.  It’s a tricky subject, because meta-awareness (and this is where it becomes rather tricky and confusing) should be so ingrained that it is nonconscious – in other words automatic.  So there’s a point in time in which the alpha and the omega, the beginning and the end meet; that which is conscious and that which is nonconscious are just really terms that we use to try to facilitate communication.  But you have to keep in mind that we don’t really have the tools, much less the terminology, to really speak of the mind. 

In progressing to mastery, the first thing the person is going to be aware of is just what processes are automatic, what processes are not, and become familiar with his or her mind.  As far as being able to describe the experiences and the ‘levels,’ that’s like trying to describe sex to a virgin – it’s just impossible.  It’s experientially based.  You go about at one level, experiencing that level, and only at that point would you have the necessary understanding to be able to comprehend what comes next.  For example, it’s not like you can describe a gymnastics routine and say ‘well you start off and you’re going to learn how to do cartwheels and round-offs and then by the end you’re going to be able to do triple pikes or what have you’ – it’s just not like that.  It’s something that you have to experience.  As I mentioned previously, the reason why we lack the terminology to describe these things is because they are experiences, and as such they are really ineffable.  What I can say with certainty is that you become a more integrated human being.  You become more aware of who you are, where you are, why you are where you are, why you’re not where you would want to be, what are the mistakes and the patterns, where they come from and how to go about changing them.  It’s a continual, never-ending process.  You never reach perfection, you just reach another newer higher level.  And of course now we’re using metaphors of space because one could also say ‘deeper’ and still have the same idea in mind.  You reach a more sophisticated, if you will, a more profound overarching over-reaching understanding of who you are and what you are, and what’s important to you.  So it is never-ending, much like Kaizen is never ending.  Constant improvement.
