The Illusion of Control

If you were to ask me "what is Existential Strategy?" and I responded with "It is the Tao of MAMBA-Ryu" then this answer would be neither informative nor helpful, for it would simply postpone the inevitable and still beg the further question: "what is the Tao of MAMBA-Ryu?" Language itself stands in the way of experiential communication: experience is multidimensional, it occurs at many levels, conscious and non-conscious alike - and in real time. Language is linear, sequential. Therein lies the first problem. If you ask me what an apple tastes like when you have never even seen an apple I will admittedly be at a loss; if you ask me what it feels like to be in love and you have never cherished another person so much that the mere thought of being without them for any length of time causes a ripping, tearing sensation in your chest, then my best attempt at an explication will be wanting at best, and at worst fail miserably. I recall being 12 years of age and asking my father what was the big deal about sex. I also recall the look of disgust on his face when he turned to me and said "ask me again in five years," and realized that I must have just asked something very stupid: When it comes to the most important aspects of life there is simply no substitute for experience. So when I tell you that Existential Strategy is coping with the absurdity of life without falling victim to the illusion of control you may not have a clue as to what I am saying, or you may get it in an instant.

Absurdity is what most characterizes our world, especially our postmodern world. As a hypnotherapist and as an intern in clinical psychology I have dealt with the effects of this absurdity on a daily basis for years. I have and will argue that the mass consumption of mind-numbing and altering substances that characterizes the postmodern world in general and the capital of the first world - the United States - in particular is simply an attempt to make ourselves insane enough so that the absurdity of the world becomes less overwhelming, less apparent, less threatening and almost normal. You are not feeling, much less aware of anything beyond the immediate rush when you are high on crystal-meth. There is no mystery as to why the United States with only 15% of the world population consumes 60% of the world production of illicit narcotics: sometimes in coping with absurdity we need all the help we can get. If you don't understand this then you have not been sober enough to witness the absurdity of a country who voted the Bush administration in for a second term.

I have spent decades of my life studying, experiencing, and healing the mind, the human condition. As a professor of the Psychology of Religion I begin my course tracing the evolution of our species, and the development of the modern mind-brain that leads to language, abstract thought, spirituality and what I coined the shamanic perspective; all manifestations of that uniquely developed human capacity we call imagination. I review with my students the research that began with my master's thesis on shamanism and culminated with the experiments of my second master's thesis and of my doctoral dissertation on imagination and hypnosis, shamanic journeying, and mysticism. I take them through the theoretical, empirical and clinical work that led me to establish that the shamanic journey and the hypnotic trance were one and the same neuro-physiological phenomena, culturally and contextually adapted. We spend a great deal of time in that course discussing the resulting anxiety that imagining the loss of our own life, the potential loss of a child, parent, job, or control creates, the role that emotional complexity plays in a social animal and how imagination can affect that emotional complexity. Therefore, when I define religion in the context of such a course of study as imagination managing imagination my students are prepared – at least intellectually – to comprehend the definition.  Yet when they ponder the consequences, the meaning, the implications of such a definition and how that impacts their lives I can see in some the joy, the freedom that this occasions and in many others the horror. There is freedom in realizing that we have no real control and that even if there are such entities as spirits, gods, goblins, devils, demons beyond manifestations of the mind-brain itself no one has ever devised a methodology to make them do our bidding in a reliable fashion. Hence the theodicy problem (aka the "Job topic"): how do we deal with the absurdity of an omnipresent, omniscient, and omnipotent deity in a world where the good suffer and the evil are not always punished? Religion is imagination managing imagination, not controlling it, not dominating it. Imagination has a mind of it own. (My course on the Psychology of Imagination – a field which I pioneered and which synthesizes work from fields seemingly as disparate as cognitive evolution, health psychology, cognitive neuro-science, clinical hypnosis, psychology of world religions, shamanism, mysticism, Eastern philosophy, Spanish literature, martial arts, and others - is dedicated to an interdisciplinary study of this very topic.)

As a professor of World Religions my task is different. I must present in a fair and objective manner the essential paradigm which each of the major religions of the world presents, all in the context of their history and evolution, emphasizing their development over time, their major changes and the causes for such. (Incidentally, to the extent to which I am objective in presenting the various religious traditions, not a single student of thousands has ever, even by the end of a semester been able to ascertain what my religious beliefs are, or even whether I am an atheist, a deist, a theist or an agnostic, a fact of which I am particularly proud and which has troubled to distraction more than one of my pupils.) But in order to establish that fair and objective standpoint in an area in which there is bound to be controversy I need to set some ground rules. To this effect, and on the very first day of classes I emphasize to my students that the single most important fact, not idea, but concrete fact to take away from the course, long after the details and the dates are forgotten, details and dates which I myself admit to having to re-remember as I prepare for the same lecture time and time again, is that all religions have three things in common: 1) all religious adherents hold their beliefs to be as dear to them as those of others, so we must respect the human value of these beliefs even if we do not agree with them; 2) all religions fail to pass the test of critical scrutiny once the evidence of the validity of their beliefs is thoroughly examined – if they did not then what would the point of faith be?; and 3) to accept that in many cases the sacred premises of one religion contradicts those of some other religious tradition in substantial and irrefutable ways.

I emphasize these three points in the context of the First Amendment of the Constitution of the United States and what is meant by freedom of religion: my right to swing my sacred beliefs ends where my neighbor's sacred nose begins. I dedicate a couple of lectures to the topic of religious fundamentalism, its definition and its social implications, and one lecture at least to religious fundamentalist terrorism. Post 9/11 and especially in the United States there is a need to fully understand that absolute religious Truths with a capital 'T' are personal values and that as socially imposable standards of conduct they are incompatible with a free society. Religious fundamentalism by definition does not refer to individuals who are committed to certain beliefs and adhere to them absolutely – this is religious orthodoxy. Religious fundamentalism encompasses the need for a social and political agenda. Religious fundamentalists are those individuals who feel that an indispensable part of their sacred duty is the obligation to impose their convictions on a social and political plane. It is less challenging to reason with one who suffers from paranoid schizophrenia about his paranoia than to make a religious fundamentalist see, let alone accept, the logical inconsistencies in his own doctrine – of this I can assure you: At least the former presents a diagnosable pathology for which medication is an option, whereas the latter is socially functional and acceptable enough to become President of the United States and commit innumerable crimes against humanity. No conflict between humans is more embittered, cruel, or implacable than when religious values are at stake or at root.

Where does that leave us? It leaves us with freedom of choice. I can choose to be a Muslim, or a Jew, or a Christian, or a Buddhist, etc., and know that I have no need to justify my faith other than by my own free choice, my own free will. But this freedom does come at a cost: one cannot resort to proof, to reason, to evidence – historical, material, or otherwise – to validate that choice for it is a choice and like most choices in life it is based on non-conscious tendencies, most of which are not justifiable or rational: Why am I in love? We have become so accustomed, so indoctrinated into believing that we are rational animals rather than rationalizing animals, that we become offended when it is explained, rationally, that the basis of our behavior is irrational. Descartes was in error.

People who do not understand something, who are ignorant of it feel threatened by it. As an African American, Native American, Spanish male who grew up largely in a Caucasian dominated society I have experienced this fact on a regular basis. Science is simply the methodology of knowledge, how to acquire it, how to decide what constitutes knowledge – what we do with knowledge once we have it is another issue. As a cognitive neuro-scientist, however, someone who has spent countless hours in the lab doing the work and seeking answers to the questions posed by the mind and brain in all its facets, rational and emotive, I am another type of minority, and for those threatened by such a fact-finding mission, I am the worst kind: a scientist dedicated to uncovering the secrets of the psyche, the mind, the soul, the seat of all that is 'the sacred' emanating from a three pound piece of flesh. Many would say this is absurd and then go straight to their psychiatrist to be prescribed their narcotic of choice to deal with their depression, come home to self-medicate by having a drink to calm their 'nerves', or bring me their child in hopes that neurofeedback will help their ADHD. As a cognitive-neuroscientist I know that perhaps the most characteristic task of the brain is to process and generate patterns. The brain, the mind, loves patterns. It detests, abhors the random, the chaotic, the unsystematic, the indiscriminant, why . . . the . . . absurd!

Accepting the absurd, dealing with the absurd, begins with recognizing the illusion of control and seeing through the control that certain illusions of culture and mind present. And if you ask me again, "what is Existential Strategy?", I will tell you what it is about: it is about transcending the absurdity of the world, of life, without succumbing to the illusion of control; it is about knowing when and how to pick and choose your own illusions while not deceiving yourself about their illusory nature; as the Chinese say: "a maker of idols is not an idolater." The difference between a fool and a wise man is that the fool experiences and accepts the illusions before him as inescapably real, whereas the wise man recognizes not just the illusions for what they are, but also experiences and accepts the inescapable reality of the fools who sustain and foment them.

J. A. GUERRA OVERTON - "THE FOUNDER"
